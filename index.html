Most of these car crashes could have been avoided

By learning about the 5 main causes of car crashes you could avoid a potentially life ending accident. The 5 main causes of road accidents include:

  • Speeding
  • Driving Under the Influence
  • Failure to wear Seatbelts
  • Fatigued Driving
  • Distracted Driving

Finding a safe vehicle

Vehicle age and safety are major factors contributing to road fatalities, and choosing a safer vehicle can greatly improve your chances of surviving a crash. Modern vehicles can even prevent a crash in the first place.

Not all cars are built equally safe

While features like front airbags are standard in most new cars, other advanced safety technologies may not be. Key safety features to consider in both new and used vehicles include:

  • Electronic Stability Control (ESC)
  • Autonomous Emergency Braking
  • Collision Avoidance Systems
  • Lane Departure Warning
  • Reverse Camera
  • Low speed collision avoidance systems
  • Blind Spot Warning

When buying a new car safety should be a priority

The Used Car Safety Ratings brochure helps you find safer models among second-hand vehicles.

Speeding

Speeds just over 5 km/h above the speed limit in urban areas, and 10 km/h above the speed limit in rural areas, are sufficient to double the risk of a crash.

Speeding is a contributing factor in about 41% of road fatalities and 24% of serious injuries each year in Australia.

Around half of all serious speeding accidents occur at less than 10km/h above the speed limit.

What can you do?

  1. Slowing Down: Research has shown that decreasing vehicle speeds by 5 km/h on roads with a speed limit of 60 km/h produced an 11.9% reduction in serious crashes. Reducing vehicle speeds by a further 5 km/h (i.e. by 10 km/h) would result in a 17.3% reduction in all serious crashes
  2. Maintaining a Safe Following Distance: Keep enough space between your car and the one in front to give yourself time to react.
  3. Checking Road Conditions: Be cautious of wet, icy, or uneven roads, which can reduce traction and increase stopping distances. As seen in the graph, wet roads considerably increase the distance to stop.
  4. Adjusting Speed to Weather: Slow down in adverse conditions such as rain, fog, or snow, where visibility and control are reduced.

Driving Under the Influence

Who is at risk?

  • Young people are the most at-risk for drunk driving. NHTSA data shows that drivers between the ages of 21-24 account for 29 percent of all fatal alcohol-impaired crashes

1 in 5 drivers killed on our roads have a blood alcohol concentration (BAC) .05 or higher.

How does alcohol affect driving

  1. Slowing down reaction time: reducing the ability to perform in an emergency.
  2. Causing poor judgement: reducing the ability to judge distances and speeds of other vehicles.
  3. Reducing attention span: reducing the ability to notice other drivers/vehicles.
  4. Causing blurred vision and reduced hearing.
  5. Creating over-confidence: drunk-driving makes you more likely to take risks.

Casualty crash risk doubles when driving with a BAC just in excess of 0.05. That's only 1 pint of beer!

What can you do?

  1. Completely separate drinking from driving: If you plan on drinking, don't drive at all. Call a taxi, use a ride-share app, or take public transport to get home.
  2. Designating a ‘dry’ driver who will not be drinking
  3. Staying overnight at a friend’s place rather than driving.

If none of these options are available

  • Males: no more than 2 standard drinks in the first hour, and no more than 1 standard drink every hour after
  • Females: no more than 1 standard drink every hour.

Seatbelts

Who is most at risk

  • Young adults (18-24 years) are less likely to wear seat belts than older adults
  • Men are less likely to wear seat belts than women and are nearly twice as likely to die in road crashes due to not wearing one.
  • Rear seat passengers are less likely to wear seat belts compared to those in the front, which increases the risk of injury to both themselves and others in the vehicle.

Why seat belts are effective

  1. Cause the occupant to decelerate at the same rate as the vehicle in a crash.
  2. Spread the force of the impact over the stronger parts of the occupant’s body (pelvis and chest area).
  3. Prevent the occupant colliding with the interior parts of the vehicle and other vehicle occupants.
  4. Reduce the risk of being thrown from the vehicle.
  5. In modern cars, seat belts are designed to work together with the airbags. In a crash, the seat belt slows the speed of the occupant, so they impact safely with the airbag.
  6. One person who does not wear a seatbelt also serves as a risk to others.

Fatigued Driving

A driver who has been awake for 17 hours has a driving ability similar to that of a driver with a blood alcohol concentration (BAC) of 0.05, and after 21 hours, similar to a BAC of 0.15

Sleepiness contributes to 20-30% of all deaths and severe injuries on the road, similar to speeding and drunk driving
